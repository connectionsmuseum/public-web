Visiting the museum

Hours

The museum is open to the public every Sunday from 10 am to 3 pm, unless otherwise noted on this page.

The suggested donation is $10 for adults and $5 youths aged 12 to 18.

While we are closed to the public for our volunteer work day, we're typically around to take donations on Tuesdays from about 11am to 3pm, but please email us in advance.

Tours

Docent guided tours start on demand, usually with little or no wait. Please note that our doors close at 2:30pm to permit visitors enough time to conclude their tour. A quick tour can take 1 hour, but typical tours take up to two hours. For those with a keen technical interest, 3 hours in the museum is common.

Returning visitors are welcome to explore the public areas of the museum on their own pace.

Directions

We are located in the Georgetown neighborhood of Seattle, Washington, at 7000 E Marginal Way S, Seattle, WA 98108. It is an active central office in a large concrete building. Our entrance is around the side, a blue door under a yellow awning.

Sadly, bike parking is limited at this time, but we are located conveniently on King County Metro Route 60 and 124, just a short walk from Stop ID #40871 and #46025.

Our parking lot entrance is on Corson Avenue. Space is often limited, but can usually be found nearby at South Seattle Community College.

Other Information

Children

We ask that parents with children under twelve visit the museum first, before bringing their children, to determine the suitability of museum exhibits for younger visitors.

Pets

We only allow trained service animals inside the museum.

Accessibility

The museum is accessible to folks using mobility aids. There is an ADA-compliant ramp leading to the elevator that will take you to the third floor to begin your tour. Please keep in mind that although the main areas are all accessible, the aisles inside the telephone switches can get quite narrow, so access may be somewhat limited, depending on your particular needs. Tours can last from 2 to 4 hours, and there is limited seating available. If you feel that you may need to take breaks, please call ahead or let your docent know, and we will gladly make arrangements for you. The telephone companies of the past proudly worked towards creating a more equitable world for people with sensory differences. Our docents are comfortabl giving tours to people who have limited sight or are hard-of-hearing. Pleae note, however, that we currently do not have anyone on staff who knows American Sign Language (ASL), so please bring along a friend to interpret for you.

Central Office (CO) Switches

Link to an online photo-gallery of our switching equipment.

Panel Switch

The 1923 Western Electric Panel Machine Switching System is the museum's pride and joy. It served telephone subscribers in Seattle for 51 years, until it was retired in 1974. It was moved to the museum in 1986, and is now the last of its kind in the entire world. Over the last 40 years at the museum, it has been lovingly restored, and visitors can place calls through it, and experience what it was like to work with this amazing machine.

No. 1 Crossbar

The No. 1 Crossbar (1XB) is the older of the museum's two crossbar switches. Installed in northeast Seattle in 1942, it was the most advanced switching technology of its day, replacing the motors and clutches of the earlier Panel system with fast-acting relays and advanced control logic. Along with its predecessor, our is the only 1XB remaining in the world. At the 1XB, visitors can try their hand at "blue boxing" an authentic telephoen switch, just like the early fone phreaks did in the 1970s.

No. 5 Crossbar

First installed in in Media, PA in 1948, the No. 5 Crossbar (5XB) was a revolution in switching technology. Although it appears similar to the 1XB on the surface, the 5XB is an entirely new machine under the hood. It features some of the most complex relay circuits ever devised, which took advantage of breakthroughs at Bell Labs by Claude Shannon and his peers. The museum's No. 5 Crossbar served customers on Mercer Island from 1958 to 1985.

Step-by-Step (SXS)

The Step-by-Step switch was the most globally successful electromechanical switching system. First invented by an undertaker from Kansas City, it was developed into a cost-effective and reliable system that provided basic telephone service for millions of people throughout the 20th Century. At the Connections Museum Seattle, visitors can dial calls through the Step and watch each selector move as their call is connected before their eyes.
